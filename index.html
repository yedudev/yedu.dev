Self-parking cars.

Building trust in autonomous valet parking through qualitative research, interaction design, and iterative prototyping.

Project hero image — Self-parking interface
Client Automotive Partner
Year 2018 – 2019
Role Lead Designer
Scope UX Research, Interaction Design, Prototyping
Overview

Designing for trust in autonomous driving.

Self-parking technology promises convenience, but the leap from manual to autonomous control requires deep trust. This project explored how interaction design can bridge that gap — making the handoff between driver and vehicle feel safe, transparent, and intuitive.

The goal was to design the end-to-end experience of an autonomous valet parking feature: activating it, leaving the car, monitoring the maneuver, and summoning the vehicle back.

Challenge

How do you hand control to a machine?

Before starting the project, user journeys had been mapped through ethnographic study. A key challenge emerged: the in-car feature activation had to be crystal clear. Users needed to understand the system state at every moment — whether conditions were met, what the car intended to do, and how to intervene.

The activation flow required checking that conditions were met (car in park gear, brake released, no hands on steering wheel), allowing users to choose to stay in or leave the car, and requiring confirmation to start the maneuver once outside.

Process image — Workshop and research artifacts
Process

From scoping workshop to parking lot testing.

The project kicked off with a three-day scoping workshop to narrow in on specific scope and define success criteria. From there, four one-hour qualitative sessions were conducted at an open, public parking lot.

Participants were walked through the full process of a self-parking car — activating it, leaving the car, monitoring it, and summoning it — and exposed to different, ambiguous prototypes to gauge reactions and uncover expectations.

Solution

Recognizable patterns that build confidence.

The final design was derived from user tests and extensive discussions on using recognizable interaction patterns. Instead of inventing entirely new paradigms, the solution leveraged familiar UI conventions — adapted to the unique context of autonomous parking.

Clear system status indicators, step-by-step confirmation flows, and a persistent monitoring interface gave users the confidence to trust the vehicle. The design was demonstrated at CES 2019, featuring the autonomous valet parking solution along with concept videos and presentation material.

Solution detail — Mobile monitoring view
Outcome

Presented on the world stage.

The design solution was showcased at CES 2019 as part of the autonomous valet parking demonstration. The research findings informed not just the parking feature, but broader principles for human-autonomous vehicle interaction across the organization.

Digital Mobility Assistants.

Exploring the future of mobility and voice assistants for next-generation vehicles through strategic UX and concept development.

Project hero image — Mobility assistant interface
Client Automotive Innovation Lab
Year 2019 – 2021
Role UX Strategist
Scope UX Strategy, Concept Development, Prototyping
Overview

Reimagining the in-car experience.

As vehicles evolve from transportation tools to digital platforms, the relationship between driver and car is fundamentally changing. This project explored how voice assistants and digital mobility services can create calmer, more mindful driving experiences.

The work spanned concept development for sustainable driving behavior apps, simplified interior concepts, and next-generation voice interaction paradigms.

Challenge

Complexity meets calm.

Modern vehicles are packed with features, yet the driving experience should feel simple and focused. The challenge was designing digital assistants that enhance rather than distract — providing the right information at the right moment without overwhelming the driver.

Concept — Voice interaction flow
Concept — Dashboard integration
Approach

From research to concept car.

The team conducted extensive research into driver behavior, attention patterns, and the role of ambient information in vehicles. These insights shaped the design of a concept car focused on mindful driving — where technology recedes and the journey takes center stage.

Concepts ranged from apps encouraging sustainable driving habits to voice-first interfaces that minimize visual distraction while maximizing utility.

Outcome

A vision for the future of mobility.

The project delivered a comprehensive UX strategy and set of design principles for next-generation in-car experiences. The concept car and accompanying research findings were presented to senior leadership, influencing the product roadmap for future vehicle models.

Reinventing the letter.

Designing digital services and UX strategies that transform how citizens interact with public institutions.

Project hero image — Digital letter concept
Client Public Sector
Year 2016 – 2018
Role Service Designer
Scope Service Design, UX Strategy, User Research
Overview

Bridging institutions and citizens.

Government communication can feel impersonal and confusing. This project reimagined the traditional letter — transforming dense, formal correspondence into clear, accessible digital experiences that respect citizens' time and cognitive load.

The work involved mapping the full communication journey, identifying pain points, and designing solutions that serve both institutional requirements and citizen needs.

Process

Understanding the system.

Service blueprints and user journey maps revealed a web of interconnected touchpoints — from backend systems generating letters to the moment a citizen opens their digital mailbox. Each touchpoint was an opportunity to reduce friction and improve clarity.

Outcome

Clearer communication at scale.

The redesigned digital letter system introduced structured content templates, plain language guidelines, and contextual help — making government communication more human. The framework was adopted across multiple agencies, improving comprehension rates and reducing support inquiries.

Client Smart Home Platform
Year 2017 – 2018
Role UX Designer
Scope Voice UI, User Research, Workshop Facilitation, Prototyping
Overview

Conversations as interfaces.

Voice interaction strips away visual scaffolding, leaving only the conversation. This project explored how to design voice experiences that feel natural, helpful, and trustworthy — from smart home control to proactive suggestions.

Behaving expressions.

An aesthetic exploration of shape-changing forms and the expressive richness of computers as a material for design.

Project hero image — Shape-changing form exploration
Context Research Project
Year 2015 – 2016
Role Researcher & Designer
Scope Physical Computing, Research, Exploration
Overview

When materials come alive.

This research project explored the boundary between physical and digital, investigating how shape-changing interfaces can communicate emotion, state, and intention through movement alone. The work treated computation as a design material — something that can be molded, shaped, and given expressive behavior.

Exploration

Movement as meaning.

Through iterative prototyping with actuators, soft materials, and embedded sensors, the project produced a series of artifacts that express different emotional qualities through their physical behavior — from gentle breathing rhythms to sharp, attention-grabbing gestures.

The findings contributed to the growing field of shape-changing interfaces and offered new perspectives on how computational objects can relate to people on an emotional level.

Design ethos.

Great design solutions don't exist in isolation — they are part of larger systems. Understanding the full context, the people, their needs, and the environment they operate in is where meaningful design begins.

Evidence-based design

Design decisions grounded in data, research, and rational insights rather than intuition alone. Every choice answers a question, informs a decision, or validates an assumption.

Calm technology

The best design fades into the background, letting people focus on what matters. Technology should be a quiet enabler — seamless, intuitive, and unobtrusive.

Systems thinking

Using user journeys and service blueprints to map the interconnectedness of different parts of the system, creating cohesive experiences across physical and digital touchpoints.

Experimentation

Design as exploration. Prototyping quickly, testing assumptions, and iterating based on what's learned — embracing uncertainty as a path to better solutions.

Collaboration

The best work happens at the intersection of disciplines. Facilitating workshops, bridging engineering and design, and building shared understanding across teams.

Leadership

Guiding teams through ambiguity, establishing design processes, and creating the conditions where great work can emerge. Design leadership is about enabling others.
